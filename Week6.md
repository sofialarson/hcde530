## Claim Explanations
C5: To answer three research questions about Madison Park (Census Tract 63), I loaded and cleaned five ACS census CSV files using pandas, applying pd.to_numeric with errors='coerce' to handle suppressed and formatted values, and renamed to standardize inconsistent column headers across files. To answer Question 1, I used groupby and sum to combine male and female population estimates into a single total per age bracket, revealing that the 50–54 age group has the largest population at 8.4% of the tract. For Question 3, I used merge to join Madison Park and Seattle household income tables on a shared label column, then filtered rows using isin to isolate brackets above $100K and computed each area's share using aggregation — finding that 79.3% of Madison Park households earn over $100K compared to a substantially lower share in Seattle, pointing to a stark wealth concentration in Madison Park in comparison to the general Seattle area.

C6: For this analysis, I built three charts in Python using Plotly, selecting them based on the chart chooser file. For Question 1, I used a bar chart to show population by age bracket since I had many ordered categories and needed to compare them; a pie chart or scatter would have made the distribution harder to read. For Question 2, I used a pie chart to show the split between households with and without their own children under 18, since there were only two categories and the whole-to-part relationship was the point. For Question 3, I used a grouped bar chart to compare the share of households earning over $100K across income brackets for Madison Park and Seattle side by side, as a grouped bar chart would enable us to see which income brackets over $100k had the highest concentration of the population represented, whilst also still enabling us to see how the percentages per income bracket compared from Seattle to Madison Park. 

C7: When Claude initially generated the pie chart for Question 2, it hardcoded 30.4% as the percentage of households with children under 18 rather than pulling the value dynamically from my script, where the actual figure was 29.6%, with 803 households out of 2,710 total. I caught the discrepancy by cross-referencing the visualization output against my analysis script results, and pushed back on it directly before correcting the code. Beyond catching that error, I also exercised professional judgment in interpreting the limitations of the data itself. The ACS households and families table only captured households with the householder's own children under 18, which I noted in my judgments and limitations section as an incomplete picture of how many Madison Park families have children in the household at all. I flagged this distinction because presenting the 29.6% figure to a stakeholder without that caveat could lead to underestimating family-oriented engagement opportunities for the website redesign.
